The Sovereign

A Stoic Workbook for Reclaiming Your Peace When Someone Has Your Mind

A Note from the Author

This book began as a letter I wrote to myself.

I didn't know it would become something I'd share. I only knew that I needed someone to tell me — honestly, without judgment, without telling me to move on — exactly what was happening in me, and how to find my way back to myself.

I hope it does the same for you.

— Amber Harbaugh
An Invitation

Before you Begin

You did not pick this book up by accident.

Something brought you here. A conversation that wouldn't leave you alone. A pattern you have been noticing in yourself. A message you sent that you wished you hadn't, or one you waited too long for, or one that arrived and somehow did not settle anything.

A feeling in your chest that has been there for a while now.

This book is not going to tell you what to do about him. Those questions are yours, and they will become clearer in their own time.

The work of these thirty days is something else.

It is to find your way back to the part of yourself that has been quietly waiting — at the edge of his life, at the edge of an unanswered message, at the edge of a question that has not yet resolved — and bring her home.

Begin when you are ready.

An Invitation

How to Use This Book

This book is thirty days long. Each day has the same shape — a short reading, an anchor quote, a longer understanding, a practice, and an evening reflection. The whole thing takes between thirty minutes and an hour, depending on how much you write.

You can use this book in two ways. You can read it on screen and write directly into the digital workbook, where there is no limit on how much you can say. Or you can print it and write in it by hand. Whichever feels right is right.

Do one day at a time, in order. Not in batches. Not all at once. The work happens in the daily-ness of it.

If you miss a day, do not start over. Pick up where you left off.

If a day asks too much of you, you can read the chapter and skip the practice. Come back to the practice the next morning, or the weekend, or whenever you can. Reading is part of the work. The practice will keep.

Do this in the evening if you can. Somewhere quiet. With something warm to drink, if that helps. Without your phone in your hand.

Begin tomorrow, or begin tonight. There is no perfect day to start.

The first day is waiting for you.

The Mirror  ·  Day One

The Uninvited
Guest

You know the feeling.

It lives somewhere between your chest and your stomach and it doesn't have a name. It isn't exactly nausea. It isn't exactly excitement. It's both at once, wound together into something that lives quietly underneath everything you do.

It showed up one day. It lives here now.

Some days it's almost quiet. A warmth that spreads through your chest when something reminds you of him — a song, a season, a time of day that has somehow become quietly his. Other days you almost want it to go away. A pressure with nowhere to go, an urgency that makes you want to walk out of your own skin — to just answer the one quiet question that keeps arriving without warning.

You carry it with you. You carry it into conversations with people who have no idea it's there. You carry it into a room and smile, completely fine, while somewhere underneath all of this a quieter part of you is still waiting. Still wondering what he's doing right now, whether you crossed his mind today the way he crossed yours this morning.

And you know — even if it's hard to admit — that a single message from him could make all of it better in an instant. That somewhere along the way, without meaning to, you've handed him your own peace. You've handed the key to your peace to someone who doesn't even know he's holding it.

That's what this book is about.

Not getting over him. Not pretending you don't feel what you feel.

Just getting yourself back.

The Anchor
"Men are disturbed not by things, but by the opinions and notions they form concerning things."
— Epictetus, The Enchiridion

That feeling in your body is not caused by him. It is caused by what your mind has decided he means — and by the story it keeps telling in the silence between his messages.

The Understanding

There is a difference between knowing something and feeling it in your bones.

You might already suspect, on some level, that this isn't entirely about him. That the intensity of what you feel — your capacity to love, your sensitivity, the way you experience the world — says as much about how you're wired as it does about him. You might already sense that the version of him in your head is more powerful than the actual person who just hasn't replied to your text yet.

This is not a character flaw. This is your nervous system doing what it was designed to do. When someone matters to us, we become wired to watch for signs. The silence becomes its own kind of data. And so the mind keeps working the problem — analyzing messages, imagining outcomes, running possible futures — staying in a low-level anxiety that feels not quite like living. Something that keeps you stuck in waiting.

The Stoics called this the gap: the space between what happens and how you respond. Between a message not arriving and the spiral that follows. Between reality and the story your mind writes in the silence.

Part of the thinking feels reasonable — like if the analysis is thorough enough, you'll find the answer. But the mind cannot think its way out of a feeling. And the feeling, when you trace it back to its root, is not about him. It is about what your mind believes a message from him would confirm about you.

And that is a belief you can work with.

Today's Practice

Today there is no challenge. No rules, nothing to change, nothing to push through. Today there is only one thing to do, and it takes less than a minute.

At some point today — on a walk, in the shower, making coffee, lying in bed — he will appear in your mind. He always does. When he does, don't push the thought away. Don't follow it either.

Just stop for a moment. Put one hand on your chest, right where that feeling lives. Take one slow breath, all the way in and all the way out. And say quietly to yourself — out loud if you can:

"I feel this. I'm not going to chase it right now. I'm right here."

Then come back to whatever you were doing.

That's the whole practice. Every time he appears today, do exactly that. Just those three things — hand on chest, one breath, those words.

You are not trying to make the feeling go away. You are not fighting it or suppressing it. You are simply placing one hand on it and reminding yourself that you are here, in your body, in your own life.

What to Expect

It will feel too simple. The mind will want more — more analysis, more resolution, more certainty. That restlessness is not a sign that you are doing it wrong. It is exactly what you are here to observe.

Just notice how many times he appears today. Don't judge the number. Just notice it.

That noticing — quiet, without drama — is where everything begins.

Evening Reflection

Find a quiet moment before bed. Pour something warm if that helps. Answer these as the person you actually are right now — not the person you think you should be. There are no right answers here. Only honest ones.

1. Describe the feeling in your body when he is on your mind. Where does it live? What does it actually feel like? Give it words, even imperfect ones.

2. When he appeared in your mind today — what were you hoping for in that moment? Not the message. The feeling underneath the wanting. What were you reaching for?

3. When you imagine receiving the perfect message from him right now, what shifts in your body? What does that tell you about where you have placed your sense of safety?

4. What has this been costing you — in presence, in attention, in the hours of your own life? Name it specifically, without judgment.

5. What is the one thing you would do differently tomorrow if you genuinely believed his message — whatever it said — could not determine how you felt about yourself? Not what you would feel. What you would do.

You gave someone the key to your peace without realizing it —
this is how you quietly take it back.

The Mirror  ·  Day Two

The Story You're
Telling

You feel it before you even realize what you're feeling.

A small drop somewhere in your chest. A shift so subtle you almost miss it — except you never miss it. Your body has become finely tuned to this particular frequency, the way you can sense a change in weather before a single cloud appears.

You glance at your phone. Nothing.

You put it down. Pick it up again. Still nothing.

You tell yourself you're fine. You almost believe it.

And something that was okay a few minutes ago is suddenly, quietly, not okay.

The mind moves in immediately. Not to help — just to make sense of the silence. To fill it with something, anything, because uncertainty is the one thing it cannot leave alone. Maybe he's pulling back. Maybe something you said didn't land the way you meant it. Maybe he was more present last week than he is this week and you just didn't notice the shift. Maybe you've been reading this entirely wrong from the beginning.

Each thought arrives with its own small wave of feeling. And each wave makes the story feel more true. Of course something is wrong. Look at how this feels. Your body wouldn't lie to you.

But here's what actually happened.

He took a little longer to reply than usual.

That's it. That's the whole event. A message not sent, nothing said, a phone sitting quietly on a table. The facts of the situation are, on their own, completely neutral.

Everything else — the drop in your chest, the analysis, the spiral, the conclusions you drew about what it means and what he is feeling and what is going to happen now — was written by your mind.

And this is one of the most important things you will learn inside this workbook:

The feeling is real. The story is not.

The Anchor
"Men are disturbed not by things, but by the opinions and notions they form concerning things."
— Epictetus, The Enchiridion

Between what happens and what you feel about it, there is always a story. There is always a choice — even when it doesn't feel like one.

The Understanding

The Stoics made a distinction that sounds simple and turns out to be one of the most quietly radical ideas in all of human thought.

They called the gap between what actually happens and our interpretation of it the impression. What actually happened — the plain fact of it — is separate from the meaning the mind immediately layers on top. A delayed reply is a delayed reply. It is not him losing interest. It is not evidence of pulling away. It is not confirmation of anything except that a reply has not yet arrived.

Part of the thinking feels reasonable — like if you stay in the story long enough, if the analysis is thorough enough, you'll find the answer. The certainty your nervous system is looking for. But the mind cannot think its way out of a feeling. And so it keeps working the problem: re-reading messages, imagining how things might unfold, running possible futures.

The next thirty days are not about eliminating the feeling. They are about learning to pause in the gap. To notice the moment the story begins. To ask, quietly: what actually happened — and what am I adding?

That pause, practiced once a day, becomes a skill. And the skill becomes something you carry into every conversation, every silence, every morning when you wake up and reach for your phone before you reach for yourself.

Today's Practice

Today, when you feel the familiar drop — when the mind moves toward the story — pause before you follow it.

Ask yourself one question, as quietly and honestly as you can:

"What actually happened?"

Not what it might mean. Not what you're afraid it means. Just the plain fact of what occurred.

He texted late. He seemed distracted. He didn't respond the way you hoped. Write down just the fact — one clear sentence. Then notice everything else as a layer your mind added on top.

What to Expect

This will feel frustratingly simple at first. The mind wants complexity. It wants to analyze. Let it notice the impulse without feeding it. The goal today is not to stop the story. It is simply to see it.

That moment of seeing — the tiny gap between the event and your interpretation — is where your freedom lives.

Evening Reflection

Find a quiet place. No phone. Something warm if it helps. Answer honestly — not as who you think you should be, but as who you are right now.

1. Describe a moment today when you noticed the feeling before you noticed the thought. What was happening in your body before your mind got involved?

2. Identify one story you told yourself today about him — something you concluded from minimal evidence. What was the bare fact underneath it?

3. What does it feel like to sit with just the fact — without the story? Is there relief in it, or does it feel too empty? Notice both.

4. What story do you most want to be true right now? What need would that story meet if it were real?

5. If you met the feeling without the story — just sat with the feeling as a physical sensation — what would you need to give yourself right now?

The feeling is real.
The story is yours to examine.
